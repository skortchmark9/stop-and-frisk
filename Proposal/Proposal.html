Appendix

Abstract

We will use the comprehensive NYC stop-and-frisk data to analyze correlations between race, economic standing, location, and incidences of stopping-and-frisking. Because the data we are using is timestamped up-to-the-minute we will be able to track stop-and-frisk over a period of 10 years, and track significant changes in frequency or demographics against significant events, for example: the mayoral election, stop-and-frisk is declared unconstitutional, and Brown University protests then-police commissioner Ray Kelly. Using this as a base, we will integrate other data sets as well - the Civilian Complaint Review Boards Bi-Annual Listing of complaints against the police, census data about median income and demographics by district

Hypothesis

We will analyze and visualize NY stop-and-frisk data to illuminate patterns and correlations between events across different factors and times.

Problem description

Stop-and-frisk policies have been widely contested. In 2013, a New York judge ruled the city’s use of the policy unconstitutional. Yet, systems similar to New York’s are still in place in many cities within the United States. Further, these cities often lack transparency in their protocols and do not release complete, publically available stop-and-frisk data. Chicago, for example, had four times as many stop-and-frisks incidents that did not lead to an arrest as New York did at the peak use of the policy, according to a report from the ACLU. However, Chicago ‘ only records information about stops if there is no arrest or charges. Stops that result in arrest are not identifiable and so the rate of innocent persons stopped cannot be ascertained.’

Implementation plan

To get 75%

  1. We will analyze arrest data and create a classifier to determine the probability of being stopped and frisked given any combination of characteristics. Characteristics to be analyzed include sex, race, age, height, build, hair color, and eye color. This will be shown on a heat-map of New York.
  2. Color will be used to distinguish stop-and-frisks which lead to arrests, and which didn’t.
  3. Users will be able to slide through particular dates, and see how the heat map changes.

To get full credit (100%)

  1. We will add income, race, and frequency of police complaints as overlays on top of our map data.
  2. Significant dates will be displayed against the time slider, so that their potential impact on stop-and-frisks can be observed.
  3. An additional line graph will be displayed below the map, showing multiple aspects (for example, likelihood of a person matching demographic characteristics being stopped and frisked) over time.

To get 125%

Twitter data will be analyzed for sentiment towards the NYPD. This will be graphed and overlayed onto stop and frisk data on the map and line graph.

Data Sets

New York Stop and Frisk Database

Source: http://www.nyc.gov/html/nypd/html/analysis_and_planning/stop_question_and_frisk_report.shtml

The 2014 version of this dataset has 45788 data entries alone, and data is available from 2003-2014. It is organized in CSV format, with multiple, clear and deterministic categories, and will not need to be cleaned. Additionally, it contains latitude and longitude of stop-and-frisk incidents, so it will be our most fine-grained geographic data set.

Census Data: Income and Demographics

Sources: http://factfinder.census.gov/faces/tableservices/jsf/pages/productview.xhtml?pid=ACS_13_5YR_B19013&prodType=table

http://factfinder.census.gov/faces/tableservices/jsf/pages/productview.xhtml?pid=ACS_13_5YR_DP05&prodType=table

These datasets contains the median income and racial distributions of U.S. census tracts in New York State.

CCRB NYPD Complaints Listing

Source: http://www.nyc.gov/html/ccrb/html/news/reports.shtml

This dataset contains the number of reported complaints by police precinct. Precincts represent the largest area of geographic distribution we will be working with, and therefore a challenge of using this data set will be adjusting the other ones to fit.

Backup Plan

If our analysis and comparison of our data doesn’t work out, we will work on the strict visualization of our New York dataset, making it as visually appealing and informative as possible.
